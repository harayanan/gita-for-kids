# Chapter 2 — Sankhya Yoga (The Yoga of Wisdom)

## Overview
- **Verses:** 72 (longest chapter in the Gita)
- **Speakers:** Sanjaya (v1, v9-10), Arjuna (v4-8, v54), Krishna (v2-3, v11-53, v55-72)
- **Folk Art Style:** Gond (Madhya Pradesh)
- **Central Theme:** The immortality of the soul, the call to duty, equanimity in action
- **Arc:** Arjuna's grief → Krishna's teaching begins → the soul is eternal → do your duty → steady wisdom
- **Significance:** This is the foundational chapter — Krishna's first full teaching. Every major Gita concept is seeded here.

## Story Mix
~60% mythological (43), ~40% modern Indian (29)

The mythological stories dominate in the soul/atman section (v11-30) and the warrior's duty section (v31-38), since these are deeply embedded in the battlefield narrative. Modern stories appear more in the practical/psychological sections (Buddhi Yoga, Sthitaprajna).

### Recurring Modern Characters
- **Nandu** — 10-year-old boy from a village near Bhopal, Madhya Pradesh (Gond art's heartland). His thatha (grandfather) recently passed away. Nandu is processing grief, loss, and what it means to "go on." His arc mirrors Arjuna's — from paralyzed by sorrow to understanding.
- **Baa** — Nandu's paternal grandmother, a Gond painter. Weathered hands, steel spine, gentle voice. She teaches through painting and stories, not lectures. She's grieving too, but carries it differently.
- **Kabir** — Nandu's best friend, restless, impulsive, always chasing the next thing. Counterpoint for the Sthitaprajna section — what an unsteady mind looks like, and how it can learn.

### One-off Characters (already established in v1-5)
Verses 1-5 use standalone characters: Priya (swimmer, Chennai), Aisha (tabla player, Varanasi), Vivaan (child of divorce), Deepa (Madurai, grandmother's potter story). These remain as-is.

## Thematic Sections

### Section 1: Arjuna's Grief Deepens (Verses 1-10)
The chapter opens where Chapter 1 left off. Arjuna is broken. Krishna challenges him, but Arjuna pushes back — how can I fight my teachers? He'd rather beg. Finally, he surrenders to Krishna as teacher.

| Verse | Speaker | Teaching | Story Type | Story Concept |
|-------|---------|----------|------------|---------------|
| 1 | Sanjaya | Sanjaya narrates: Krishna speaks to weeping Arjuna | Mythological | *(done)* The Friend Who Stayed — Krishna holds the reins and simply stays |
| 2 | Krishna | Where has this weakness come from? This is not noble | Modern | *(done)* The Coach at Halftime — Priya's swim coach refuses to let her shrink |
| 3 | Krishna | Cast off this faint-heartedness. Arise, O scorcher of foes! | Modern | *(done)* Stand Up, Scorcher — Aisha's grandfather gives her back her name |
| 4 | Arjuna | How can I fight Bhishma and Drona with arrows? | Modern | *(done)* Arrows Against Your Own — Vivaan caught between divorcing parents |
| 5 | Arjuna | Better to beg than slay great-souled teachers | Modern | *(done)* Blood on the Prize — Deepa's grandmother's potter story |
| 6 | Arjuna | I don't know which is better — to conquer them or be conquered | Mythological | Arjuna looks across the field at Bhishma's silver armor and Drona's calm face — neither outcome feels like winning |
| 7 | Arjuna | Overcome by weakness, confused about duty — I ask You, teach me | Mythological | The turning point — Arjuna drops his pride and asks Krishna to be his teacher (guru-shishya moment) |
| 8 | Arjuna | Even lordship of the earth or heavens cannot remove this grief | Mythological | Arjuna imagines being king of everything — it means nothing if it costs this |
| 9 | Sanjaya | Having spoken thus, Arjuna says "I will not fight" and falls silent | Mythological | Sanjaya's narration — the silence after the storm; Arjuna's Gandiva lies across his knees |
| 10 | Sanjaya | Krishna, smiling, speaks to the despondent Arjuna between the armies | Mythological | The smile — not mocking, but the smile of a doctor who knows the medicine will work |

### Section 2: The Immortality of the Soul (Verses 11-25)
The great teaching begins. Krishna reveals that the soul (atman) is eternal, unborn, undying. Bodies come and go like changing clothes. This is the philosophical foundation of the entire Gita.

| Verse | Speaker | Teaching | Story Type | Story Concept |
|-------|---------|----------|------------|---------------|
| 11 | Krishna | You grieve for those who should not be grieved for; the wise grieve neither for the living nor the dead | Modern | Nandu refuses to enter Thatha's empty room — Baa says "the ones worth crying for are the ones who never truly lived" |
| 12 | Krishna | There was never a time when I did not exist, nor you, nor these kings — nor will there be a time when we cease to exist | Mythological | Krishna gestures at the armies — Bhishma, Drona, Arjuna — all of them have existed forever, in forms beyond counting |
| 13 | Krishna | As the soul passes through childhood, youth, and old age in this body, so it passes into another body — the wise are not deluded | Modern | Baa shows Nandu old photos — baby Thatha, young Thatha, old Thatha — "which one is the real him?" she asks. All of them. None of them. |
| 14 | Krishna | Contact of the senses with their objects gives rise to cold/heat, pleasure/pain — they are temporary, endure them | Modern | Nandu burns his hand on a hot tawa; Baa doesn't panic — "it hurts now. Will it hurt in a year?" The lesson of impermanence in sensation |
| 15 | Krishna | The person whom these do not disturb, who is equal in pleasure and pain — that one is fit for immortality | Mythological | A sage sitting unmoved while a storm rages around him — not because he can't feel it, but because he knows it will pass |
| 16 | Krishna | The unreal has no existence; the real never ceases to be | Mythological | Krishna draws a line in the sand of the battlefield — on one side, everything that changes; on the other, the one thing that doesn't |
| 17 | Krishna | Know that which pervades the entire body is indestructible — no one can destroy the imperishable soul | Modern | Baa's Gond painting — she paints Krishna's face, then washes the cloth. "The paint is gone. Is Krishna gone?" |
| 18 | Krishna | Only the body is perishable; the soul is eternal, immeasurable, indestructible — therefore fight | Mythological | Krishna's voice changes — this is not philosophy anymore, it's a command. He points at the armies: "These bodies will end. The beings within them will not." |
| 19 | Krishna | One who thinks the soul kills, one who thinks it is killed — both are ignorant. It neither kills nor is killed | Mythological | An ancient teaching story — a king who mourned a broken pot, and the sage who said "did the sky inside the pot die?" |
| 20 | Krishna | The soul is never born, never dies, never having been it ceases to be — unborn, eternal, primeval, it is not slain when the body is slain | Mythological | The most famous verse — Krishna speaks it slowly, each word landing like a stone in still water. The chariot seems to glow. |
| 21 | Krishna | How can one who knows the soul is indestructible cause anyone to be slain? | Mythological | Krishna's logic, gentle but relentless — if you truly understand verse 20, what exactly are you afraid of destroying? |
| 22 | Krishna | As a person casts off worn-out clothes and puts on new ones, so the soul casts off worn-out bodies and enters new ones | Modern | Nandu finds Thatha's old kurtas in a trunk. Baa says "He changed his clothes. That's all." — the metaphor that has comforted millions |
| 23 | Krishna | Weapons cannot cut it, fire cannot burn it, water cannot wet it, wind cannot dry it | Mythological | Krishna lists the elements one by one — each vast, each powerful, each helpless against the soul. Arjuna listens. |
| 24 | Krishna | This soul is unbreakable, insoluble, cannot be burned or dried — it is eternal, all-pervading, stable, immovable, primeval | Mythological | The cumulative weight of adjectives — Krishna is stacking words like stones, building a wall between Arjuna and his grief |
| 25 | Krishna | The soul is invisible, unthinkable, immutable — knowing this, you should not grieve | Modern | Nandu asks Baa "but where IS Thatha now?" — Baa says "close your eyes. Think of his voice. Can you hear it? That's not memory. That's him." |

### Section 3: Beyond Grief (Verses 26-30)
Even if you don't accept the soul's eternity — even then, grief is futile. Birth and death are natural. The soul is wondrous.

| Verse | Speaker | Teaching | Story Type | Story Concept |
|-------|---------|----------|------------|---------------|
| 26 | Krishna | Even if you think the soul is constantly born and dies — still you should not grieve | Mythological | Krishna concedes the argument for a moment — "Even in YOUR framework, Arjuna, grief doesn't work" |
| 27 | Krishna | For the born, death is certain; for the dead, birth is certain — do not grieve for the inevitable | Modern | Baa at the temple — she lights a lamp, watches it burn down, lights another. "Do you cry when Tuesday becomes Wednesday?" |
| 28 | Krishna | Beings are unmanifest before birth, manifest in the middle, unmanifest again after death — what is there to grieve? | Mythological | Krishna sweeps his hand across the battlefield — before this war, these warriors were unborn. After, they will be unmanifest again. The visible part is the smallest part. |
| 29 | Krishna | Some see the soul as a wonder, some speak of it as a wonder, some hear of it as a wonder — and some, even hearing, do not understand | Mythological | A moment of awe — Krishna pauses, and his voice fills with something like reverence for the mystery of the atman itself |
| 30 | Krishna | The soul in everyone's body is eternally indestructible — therefore you should not grieve for any being | Mythological | The teaching lands. Krishna looks at Arjuna. "Every single being on that field — your side and theirs — carries something that cannot be harmed." |

### Section 4: The Warrior's Dharma (Verses 31-38)
Krishna shifts from philosophy to duty. As a Kshatriya, Arjuna must fight. And practically speaking — he wins heaven either way.

| Verse | Speaker | Teaching | Story Type | Story Concept |
|-------|---------|----------|------------|---------------|
| 31 | Krishna | Considering your own dharma as a warrior, you should not waver — there is nothing better for a Kshatriya than a righteous war | Mythological | Krishna names it — this isn't just any battle, it's dharma-yuddha. Arjuna's whole life was training for this moment. |
| 32 | Krishna | Happy are the warriors who get such a battle unsought — it opens the gates of heaven | Mythological | Krishna describes the rare gift — most warriors pray their whole lives for a righteous cause. This one came to Arjuna's door. |
| 33 | Krishna | If you do not fight this righteous war, you will abandon your duty and fame, and incur sin | Modern | Kabir was supposed to stand up for a bullied classmate. He stayed quiet. The guilt followed him home. |
| 34 | Krishna | People will speak of your eternal dishonor — for one who has been honored, dishonor is worse than death | Mythological | Krishna paints the picture — whispers in court, songs that mock, a name that becomes a byword for cowardice |
| 35 | Krishna | Great warriors will think you fled from battle out of fear — those who esteemed you will hold you in contempt | Mythological | Bhishma and Drona looking at an empty chariot. The silence of their disappointment. |
| 36 | Krishna | Your enemies will speak unspeakable words about you, scorning your strength — what could be more painful? | Mythological | Duryodhana laughing. The Kaurava soldiers pointing. The humiliation not just of Arjuna but of everyone who believed in him. |
| 37 | Krishna | Slain, you gain heaven; victorious, you enjoy the earth — either way, arise and fight | Mythological | Krishna's devastating logic: there is no losing move. The only losing move is not to play. |
| 38 | Krishna | Treating pleasure and pain, gain and loss, victory and defeat alike — fight! You shall incur no sin | Modern | Nandu at a kite festival — his kite rises, dips, crashes, rises again. Baa says "you flew the kite the same way whether it was winning or losing" |

### Section 5: Buddhi Yoga — The Yoga of Intellect (Verses 39-53)
Krishna introduces the practical method. Act with a resolute mind. Don't chase results. This is yoga.

| Verse | Speaker | Teaching | Story Type | Story Concept |
|-------|---------|----------|------------|---------------|
| 39 | Krishna | This is Sankhya wisdom; now hear the wisdom of Yoga (Buddhi Yoga) — with this understanding, you will cast off the bondage of action | Mythological | A shift in Krishna's tone — from "here's why you shouldn't grieve" to "here's how to live." The teaching deepens. |
| 40 | Krishna | In this path, no effort is wasted, no loss occurs — even a little of this practice saves one from great fear | Modern | Nandu starts a Gond painting with Baa. He barely finishes one lotus petal before dinner. "Is that enough?" "Every petal counts." |
| 41 | Krishna | The resolute intellect is one-pointed; the thoughts of the irresolute are many-branched and endless | Modern | Kabir at a sweet shop — he wants jalebi, no gulab jamun, no rasmalai — he can't choose and ends up with nothing |
| 42 | Krishna | The undiscerning who delight in flowery words of the Vedas say "there is nothing else" | Mythological | Krishna warns against people who use scripture like a shopping list — reciting rituals not for wisdom but for heaven and power |
| 43 | Krishna | Full of desires, intent on heaven, they perform elaborate rituals for pleasure and power — their minds are stolen by such talk | Mythological | Continuation — the priests who promise "do this ritual, get that reward" — Krishna says the Gita goes deeper |
| 44 | Krishna | Those whose minds are carried away by these — their resolute intellect is not established in samadhi | Mythological | The flowery-worded preachers have followers, but those followers never reach stillness — always wanting, never resting |
| 45 | Krishna | The Vedas deal with the three gunas — be free of them, O Arjuna, be established in sattva, beyond duality | Mythological | Krishna doesn't reject the Vedas — he says "graduate." The Vedas are the school; the Gita is the field. |
| 46 | Krishna | As useful as a small well when there is a flood everywhere — so are the Vedas to an enlightened Brahmin | Modern | A well in Nandu's village during monsoon — water everywhere. "Why would you lower a bucket when the rain is on your face?" |
| 47 | Krishna | You have a right to action alone, never to its fruits — do not let the fruits be your motive, nor let there be attachment to inaction | Mythological | The most quoted verse of the Gita. Krishna speaks it simply, without drama. The battlefield goes quiet, as if the wind itself is listening. |
| 48 | Krishna | Perform action established in yoga, abandoning attachment, equal in success and failure — equanimity is yoga | Mythological | Krishna defines yoga for the first time — not headstands, not incense, but equanimity. Samatvam yoga uchyate. |
| 49 | Krishna | Action done for results is far inferior to action done with equanimity — seek refuge in the intellect; pitiful are those motivated by results | Modern | Baa's painting: she has a commissioned Gond and one she paints "for Krishna." Guess which one has more life in it? |
| 50 | Krishna | One with an equanimous intellect casts off both good and bad results — strive for yoga; yoga is skill in action | Modern | Nandu watches a street musician play — no hat out for coins, no audience, just playing. "That's yoga," Baa says |
| 51 | Krishna | Wise ones, with equanimous intellect, renounce the fruits of action and are freed from the bondage of rebirth | Mythological | A chain of beings — each one bound to the next life by the weight of what they wanted. Krishna shows how to break the chain. |
| 52 | Krishna | When your intellect crosses the thicket of delusion, you will become indifferent to what has been heard and what is yet to be heard | Mythological | Krishna describes a forest — dense, tangled, dark. Then a clearing. You don't get to the clearing by studying maps of the forest. |
| 53 | Krishna | When your intellect, confused by scripture, stands firm and steady in samadhi — then you will attain yoga | Mythological | The chapter's first half closes — Krishna has built the foundation. Now Arjuna has a question. |

### Section 6: Sthitaprajna — The Person of Steady Wisdom (Verses 54-72)
Arjuna asks: what does an enlightened person look like? How do they sit, speak, walk? Krishna's answer is one of the Gita's most beautiful passages.

| Verse | Speaker | Teaching | Story Type | Story Concept |
|-------|---------|----------|------------|---------------|
| 54 | Arjuna | What is the description of one of steady wisdom, established in samadhi? How does such a one speak, sit, walk? | Mythological | Arjuna's beautiful question — not "how do I get there?" but "what does it look like when someone has arrived?" |
| 55 | Krishna | When one gives up all desires of the mind and is content in the Self alone — that one is of steady wisdom | Modern | Baa on a winter evening — no TV, no phone, sitting on the veranda with a blanket and a cup of chai. Nandu asks if she's bored. She laughs. |
| 56 | Krishna | Undisturbed by sorrow, free from longing for pleasure, free from attachment, fear, and anger — that sage has steady wisdom | Mythological | Krishna paints the portrait — not a stone statue, but a human being who has made peace with the full range of experience |
| 57 | Krishna | One who is without attachment everywhere, who neither rejoices nor hates when obtaining good or evil — that one has steady wisdom | Modern | Baa receives news: her painting won a state award. She nods, finishes her tea. Later, a painting is rejected. Same nod. Same tea. |
| 58 | Krishna | When one withdraws the senses from sense objects as a tortoise withdraws its limbs — that one has steady wisdom | Modern | Nandu and Kabir at the mela (fair) — Kabir is pulled in every direction (rides, sweets, toys). Nandu tries to focus on finding Baa's stall. The tortoise metaphor. |
| 59 | Krishna | Sense objects turn away from the abstinent, but the taste (rasa) lingers — even that taste ceases when one sees the Supreme | Mythological | A fasting sage — food is gone, but the memory of taste remains. Only when he touches something greater does even the memory dissolve. |
| 60 | Krishna | The turbulent senses forcibly carry away the mind even of a wise person who is striving | Modern | Kabir during exams — he knows he should study but his phone keeps pulling him. Even smart kids lose this battle. |
| 61 | Krishna | Restraining all senses, one should sit focused on Me — for one whose senses are under control, wisdom is steady | Mythological | A warrior in meditation before battle — not running from the world, but sitting in the center of it with perfect control |
| 62 | Krishna | When one thinks of sense objects, attachment arises; from attachment, desire; from desire, anger | Mythological | The chain reaction — Krishna traces it link by link. One stray thought, and the whole chain begins to form. |
| 63 | Krishna | From anger comes delusion; from delusion, loss of memory; from loss of memory, destruction of intellect; from that, one perishes | Modern | Kabir's spiral: one angry comment online → obsessive checking → lost sleep → failed test → blamed everyone else. The chain in modern form. |
| 64 | Krishna | But the self-controlled, moving among objects with senses under control, free from attraction and aversion — attains peace | Modern | Baa at the bazaar — surrounded by beautiful fabrics, sweets, noise. She buys only what she came for. Not because she can't see the beauty — because she's at peace without it. |
| 65 | Krishna | In that peace, all sorrows are destroyed — for the serene-minded, the intellect soon becomes steady | Mythological | A lake after the wind dies — the surface becomes still, and for the first time you can see all the way to the bottom |
| 66 | Krishna | There is no wisdom for the unsteady, no meditation for the unsteady, no peace for the unmeditative — and for the peaceless, how can there be happiness? | Modern | Nandu can't sleep — his mind races from thought to thought. Baa sits by his bed: "A spinning top looks like it's standing still. But it can't hold anything." |
| 67 | Krishna | The mind that follows wandering senses carries away wisdom as wind carries a ship on water | Mythological | A ship on the ocean — sails caught by every shifting wind, blown off course. The senses are the wind. The mind is the sail. |
| 68 | Krishna | Therefore, one whose senses are completely restrained from their objects — that one's wisdom is steady | Mythological | The ship again — but now with a steady hand on the rudder. Same wind. Same sea. Different outcome. |
| 69 | Krishna | What is night to all beings, there the disciplined one is awake; what is day to all beings, that is night to the seeing sage | Modern | The village sleeps. Baa is awake, painting by lamplight. Nandu finds her. "Everyone is sleeping." "Everyone is sleeping through their lives, too," she says gently. |
| 70 | Krishna | As the ocean, filled by waters entering from all sides, remains still — so one whom desires enter without disturbing attains peace, not the desirer of desires | Mythological | The great ocean metaphor — rivers pour in endlessly but the ocean never overflows. The sthitaprajna is like this. |
| 71 | Krishna | One who gives up all desires, acts without longing, without "mine-ness," without ego — attains peace | Modern | Nandu helps Baa clean her painting studio. He doesn't ask "what do I get?" He just helps. The room feels different after. Lighter. |
| 72 | Krishna | This is the state of Brahman, O Partha — attaining this, one is never deluded again. Established in this even at the hour of death, one attains Brahma-nirvana | Mythological | The chapter's final verse. Krishna's voice goes quiet. The battlefield seems very far away. Arjuna sits still for the first time. Something has changed. |

## Glossary Terms to Add (Chapter 2)

| Term | Definition |
|------|-----------|
| atman | The individual soul — the real you that lives inside the body |
| deha | The physical body — the "clothing" the soul wears |
| sankhya | A way of knowing through wisdom and reasoning |
| buddhi | The intellect — the part of your mind that decides and discerns |
| sthitaprajna | One whose wisdom is steady and unshaken |
| samatva | Equanimity — being balanced in success and failure |
| guna | One of three qualities of nature (sattva, rajas, tamas) that shape everything |
| kshatriya | A warrior — one whose dharma is to protect and fight for justice |
| yoga | Union; here, the practice of acting with a steady, focused mind |
| rasa | Taste or essence — the lingering pull of past pleasures |
| samadhi | Deep absorption — the state where the mind is perfectly still |
| nirvana | Liberation — freedom from the cycle of birth and death |
| prajna | Wisdom — deep understanding, not just knowledge |
| kaśmala | Weakness or dejection — the faint-heartedness that overcomes Arjuna |

## Thematic Notes

- **Gond's devotional stillness** suits this chapter perfectly — Krishna as the central teaching figure, surrounded by ornamental detail. The soul's immortality section (v11-25) should feel temple-like, sacred, still.
- **The clothes metaphor** (v22) is the chapter's most famous image — Gond's textile-rich aesthetic makes this a natural visual.
- **Sthitaprajna passage** (v54-72) is the Gita's first extended portrait of the ideal person. It should build gradually, each verse adding one brushstroke.
- **Tone:** Moves from urgent (v1-10) through awe-inspiring (v11-25) to practical (v31-53) to deeply calm (v54-72). The chapter ends in stillness.
- **Nandu's arc:** From refusing to enter his grandfather's room (v11) through understanding the clothes metaphor (v22) to sleepless questioning (v66) to quietly helping Baa (v71) — a child's journey through grief to acceptance.
- **Baa's teaching style:** She never lectures. She shows. She paints. She asks questions. She waits. This mirrors Krishna's own approach in the Sthitaprajna section.
- **Kabir as foil:** Not a villain — just a kid who hasn't learned self-mastery yet. His struggles (v41, v58, v60, v63) make the teaching concrete. He's the "what happens when the senses run wild" example.
