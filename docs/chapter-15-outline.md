# Chapter 15 — Purushottama Yoga (The Yoga of the Supreme Self)

## Overview
- **Verses:** 20
- **Speaker:** Krishna (entire chapter)
- **Folk Art Style:** Kalamkari (Andhra Pradesh)
- **Central Metaphor:** The cosmic Ashvattha (fig) tree — roots above, branches below
- **Climax:** Krishna reveals himself as Purushottama — beyond both the perishable and imperishable

## Story Mix
~70% mythological (14), ~30% modern Indian (6)

### Recurring Modern Characters
- **Meera** — 10-year-old girl who loves gardening and nature, lives in a village in Andhra Pradesh
- **Tara** — Meera's grandmother (Nani), retired school teacher who sees the divine in everyday things

## Verse-by-Verse Plan

### Section 1: The Cosmic Tree (Verses 1-4)

| Verse | Teaching | Story Type | Story Concept |
|-------|----------|------------|---------------|
| 1 | The inverted Ashvattha tree — roots above, branches below, leaves are the Vedas | Mythological | Krishna and Arjuna standing before a vision of the cosmic tree; Arjuna sees the universe in its branches |
| 2 | Branches spread up and down, nourished by gunas, sense-objects as buds, karma-roots binding in the human world | Mythological | Arjuna watches the tree grow — each branch a desire, each bud a pleasure, roots tangling around human figures |
| 3 | The tree's true form cannot be perceived; cut it with the axe of detachment | Mythological | A sage (Vyasa or Narada) teaching a young student about the invisible tree — the moment of wielding the axe of vairagya |
| 4 | Seek the supreme abode from which there is no return — surrender to the Primeval Being | Mythological | The forest beyond the fallen tree — a luminous clearing, the first Purusha, the source of all creation |

### Section 2: The Supreme Abode & The Soul's Journey (Verses 5-11)

| Verse | Teaching | Story Type | Story Concept |
|-------|----------|------------|---------------|
| 5 | Free from pride, delusion, attachment — such wise ones reach the eternal abode | Modern | Meera's Nani (Tara) giving away her prized possessions before a move — letting go with joy, not sadness |
| 6 | Neither sun, moon, nor fire illuminates that abode — it is self-luminous | Mythological | Arjuna glimpses Krishna's abode — a place of light without any source, where shadows don't exist |
| 7 | Living beings are eternal fragments of Me — they struggle with the six senses | Modern | Meera finds a shard of mirror reflecting the whole sky — Nani explains that each soul is a fragment of the divine |
| 8 | The soul carries the mind and senses from body to body, like wind carrying fragrance | Mythological | A dying warrior on the battlefield — his soul departing like wind sweeping through a garden, carrying the scent of jasmine |
| 9 | The soul presides over the senses and enjoys sense-objects | Modern | Meera at a village market — overwhelmed by colors, sounds, smells — Nani asks "who is the one enjoying all this?" |
| 10 | The deluded cannot see the soul; only those with eyes of knowledge can | Mythological | Two sages watching a butterfly emerge from a cocoon — one sees only the insect, the other sees the atman's journey |
| 11 | Striving yogis perceive the soul within; the unpurified cannot | Mythological | A young rishi meditating by the Ganga at dawn — the moment the inner light flickers visible for the first time |

### Section 3: Divine Light & Sustenance (Verses 12-15)

| Verse | Teaching | Story Type | Story Concept |
|-------|----------|------------|---------------|
| 12 | The radiance in sun, moon, and fire is Mine | Modern | Meera watching sunrise, moonrise, and her Nani lighting the evening lamp — the same light in all three |
| 13 | Entering the earth, I sustain all beings; as the moon, I nourish all plants | Modern | Meera and Nani in their garden — the mystery of how a tiny seed becomes a towering plant, the sap rising like a prayer |
| 14 | As digestive fire (Vaishvanara), I digest the four kinds of food | Modern | Family dinner at Meera's house — Nani explains the fire in the belly, the sacred act of eating |
| 15 | I am seated in all hearts; from Me come memory, knowledge, and forgetfulness | Mythological | Krishna on the battlefield, placing his hand on his own chest, then Arjuna's — revealing the same presence in both |

### Section 4: The Purushottama Revelation (Verses 16-20)

| Verse | Teaching | Story Type | Story Concept |
|-------|----------|------------|---------------|
| 16 | Two Purushas: the perishable (all beings) and the imperishable (the unchanging soul) | Mythological | Krishna draws two circles in the dust — one that the wind can erase, one that remains; Arjuna watches |
| 17 | The Supreme Person (Uttama Purusha / Paramatma) pervades and sustains all three worlds | Mythological | The battlefield falls silent; Krishna's form shimmers — not the cosmic form of Ch11, but a quiet, all-pervading presence |
| 18 | I transcend the perishable and the imperishable — I am Purushottama | Mythological | The naming moment — Arjuna understands why Krishna is called Purushottama; the word itself becomes a revelation |
| 19 | One who knows Me without delusion as Purushottama worships Me with complete devotion | Mythological | Arjuna's eyes fill with tears of understanding — not grief (as in Ch1) but recognition; he sees Krishna truly for the first time |
| 20 | This is the most secret teaching — understanding this, one becomes truly wise | Mythological | Krishna falls silent; the wind picks up again on Kurukshetra; the teaching is complete; Arjuna holds it in his heart like a lamp |

## Thematic Notes

- **The Tree of Life motif** in Kalamkari art maps perfectly to the Ashvattha metaphor. Illustrations should lean heavily into vine/scroll patterns.
- **Light** is a recurring image — self-luminous abode (v6), sun/moon/fire (v12), inner lamp (v15). Stories should build this thread.
- **Meera's garden** connects the abstract "I sustain all beings" to a child's tangible experience of growing things.
- **The closing verses (16-20)** should feel like a crescendo — each mythological story building toward the quiet revelation. Not dramatic like Ch11's cosmic form, but intimate and still.
- **Tone:** Wonder and quiet awe. Ch12 was warm and devotional. Ch15 should feel like standing in a forest at dawn — vast, still, luminous.
